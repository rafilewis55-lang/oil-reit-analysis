The Short Version

Oil prices don't really matter for REITs vs the S&P. When oil spikes or crashes, REITs and the S&P move about the same. There's no statistically meaningful connection.

Long-term interest rates are what actually matters. When the 10-year Treasury rate rises by 1 percentage point in a month, REITs tend to trail the S&P by about 5%. REITs act like bonds in this way.

Oil does push rates around (a little). Big oil moves tend to nudge the 10-year rate in the same direction — likely through inflation expectations — but it's a noisy relationship.

How to read these tables
  • Effect Size (coefficient): How much the REIT-minus-S&P return changes when this factor goes up by 1 unit. Negative = REITs do worse.
  • Uncertainty (std error): How imprecise the estimate is. Smaller = more reliable.
  • p-value: Probability this result is just random noise. Below 0.05 = statistically significant.
  • Stars: *** = very significant (p<0.01), ** = significant (p<0.05), * = marginal (p<0.1)
  • "Explains X% of variation": What fraction of REIT vs S&P monthly swings these factors account for. Higher = better model.

Methodology
  • REIT Index: MSCI US REIT Index (^RMZ, 1995-2021) spliced with iShares US Real Estate ETF (IYR, 2000-present)
  • Oil: WTI Crude spot (FRED: DCOILWTICO), monthly average, as % change
  • Rates: 3-Month T-Bill (FRED: DTB3) and 10-Year Treasury (FRED: DGS10)
  • Main model uses rate changes (month-over-month) rather than levels, which is more appropriate for return regressions
  • Winsorized models trim the top/bottom 1% of returns to reduce outlier influence (COVID, GFC)
  • Excel formulas: Correlation tab uses CORREL(), regression tab uses INDEX(LINEST()) — all reference the Data tab directly
